Blog Post Reflection

In lab 5 of automated composition, I had a challenging yet rewarding experience for both Part I and Part II. In this blog post, I will be discussing my experience with implementing Magenta into Lab2 and also why and how I implemented Markov Chain Learning to the tune of Twinkle Twinkle Little Star.

Part I was challenging in that I was unfamiliar with Magenta.js. Hence, it took me a while to understand how to translate certain properties into elements within Lab2. The hardest part that I struggled with was adding the offset value into the note.startTime and note.endTime. Prior to adding the offset, my oscillators were not producing any notes. Another part that I was having trouble with was understanding why the oscillators in AM and FM continuously played even after the melody of TWINKLE_TWINKLE had ended. To fix the issue, I had to set the gain value to zero immediately after the attack.

From the difficulties in Part I, I felt more comfortable with automated composition and decided to tackle Markov Chain Learning. The hardest part of Markov Chain Learning was understanding how to generate the new note onto the Twinkle Twinkle melody. After producing the bigrams of the notes, I used Math.random() to randomly select what note should be produced next. Inspired by this post, I learned that by using Math.random, I can save space since the random function imitates the likelihood of the pitch. Currently, my Markov Chain Learning adds one new pitch onto the current Twinkle Twinkle Little Star Melody.
